Church of the Company fire (Santiago, Chile) Dec. 8, 1863

More than 2,000 killed

More than 2,000 people perished in a fire at a Jesuit church in the Chilean capital on Dec. 8, 1863, the New York Times reported shortly after the disaster.

(Canadian Press)

Halifax explosion, Dec. 6, 1917

2,000 killed

On Dec. 6, 1917, a collision involving a ship carrying munitions in Halifax Harbour led to the biggest man-made explosion in the world before the era of the atomic bomb. The blast killed 2,000 people, wounded another 9,000, levelled most of the city and sent shards of glass and burning debris flying for kilometres.

Courrières mine disaster, France, 1906

1,099 killed

An explosion at a mine in northern France, which was owned by the Courrières mining firm, killed nearly 1,100 people on March 10, 1906. Among the survivors was a group of miners who escaped the mine 20 days after the blast.

Bangladeshi Army personel stand as they continue the second phase of a rescue operation using heavy equipment after an eight-storey building collapsed in Savar, on the outskirts of Dhaka.

(Munir Uz Zaman/AFP/Getty Images)

Collapse of the Rana Plaza, Savar Upazila, Bangladesh, April 24, 2013

More than 1,000 killed

At least 1,038 people died after a building complex that housed a number of garment factories collapsed near the Bangladeshi capital. It was the country's worst industrial accident, and the deadliest in the history of the global garment industry.

Benxihu Colliery explosion, China, 1942

1,549 killed

Gas exploded in a shaft at the Benxihu coal mine on April 26, 1942, killing more than 1,500 Chinese miners.

Soldiers guard the entrance of Union Carbide factory in Bhopal on Dec. 4, 1984.

Bhopal disaster, India, 1984

At least 2,000 killed

Between 2,000 and 3,500 people died immediately when 40 tonnes of lethal methyl isocyanate gas leaked from the Union Carbide pesticide plant in Bhopal, India, on the night of Dec. 3, 1984.

However, government records say the total death toll from the tragedy is about 15,000, factoring in all those who died of leak-related diseases and injuries since 1984, but local victims' rights activists say the figure is at least double that.

Chornobyl disaster, Ukraine, 1986

At least 4,056 killed

Soon after midnight on April 26, 1986, loud explosions came from Unit 4 of the Chernobyl nuclear power plant in Ukraine. It was the result of an accident, a calamitous breakdown in communications.

The International Atomic Energy Agency's Chornobyl Forum said at least 4,000 people died, or would die, from radiation-induced cancers. The World Health Organization later upped that figure to 9,000. But other estimates are much higher.

Sinking of the SS Kiangya, off Shanghai, 1948

2,750–3,920 killed

A passenger steam ship carrying refugees from China's civil war exploded north of Shanghai, killing an unknown number of passengers and crew. The ship's manifest listed 2,150 passengers, but death toll estimates in the sinking range upwards of 4,000.

Stampede by pilgrims inside a pedestrian tunnel in Mecca, 1990

1,426 killed

The deadliest accident during the annual Muslim hajj pilgrimage in Saudi Arabia took place in 1990, when 1,426 people were killed in a stampede.

(Central Press/Getty Images)

Sinking of The Titanic, North Atlantic, 1912

1,517 killed

The Titanic was the world's largest and most luxurious ocean liner when it hit an iceberg and sank on April 15, 1912, killing more than 1,500 people.

Roughly 700 people survived, most of whom were rescued by another ship that responded to the Titanic's distress call.

(GeoEye/AP)

Fukushima nuclear disaster, 2011

The earthquake and tsunami that struck Japan in March 2011 severely damaged nuclear reactors at the Fukushimi Daiichi nuclear power plant. This led to the first Level 7 nuclear meltdown since Chornobyl.

(AP)

Texas City Disaster, 1947

581 killed

Approximately 2,100 tonnes of ammonium nitrate detonated when a fire broke out on a boat docked in Texas City's port. The casualties included all but one member of the city's fire department.

(U.S. Coast Guard/Getty Images)

Deepwater Horizon oil spill, 2010

11 killed

Eleven people were killed when an explosion rocked the Deepwater Horizon oil rig in the Gulf of Mexico. The resulting spill caused more than 4.9 million barrels of oil to flood into the ocean.

(Chris Wilkins/AFP/Getty Images)

Exxon Valdez oil spill

Between 260,000 and 750,000 barrels of oil spilled into the ocean when the Exxon Valdez oil tanker ran aground in Alaska in 1989. The U.S. government estimated that in 2010, more than 87,000 litres of oil was still in the soil near where the spill occurred.

Sinking of the Toya Maru, Tsugaru Strait, 1954

1,150 killed

The Toya Maru ferry disaster killed between 1,150 and 1,170 passengers and crew, according to estimates. The ship sank in the Tsugaru Strait, between the coasts of Japan and the Korean peninsula, while it was at sail during a typhoon.
